Let me talk about something that I think is a little underappreciated about climbing - climbing things you suck at.


### Nobody likes to climb things that they suck at

People enjoy being good at something. If I climb a V5 in crimpy slab, but I climb only a V3 in overhang - I'm going to want to take a stab at the slab a lot more than I'm going to want to try the V3. 

### Ego

Ego is a huge thing when it comes to climbing - and things that come more naturally to you will often garner praise. ("Oh my god I can't believe that worked!" - in an awestruck tone). (If I'm going to be honest, this is one of the major detractions of climbing with friends - is being too influenced by one's ego, but I digress and will talk about that in a separate post). 

Nobody enjoys failing on a move that their climbing friends succeed at. Even though the adage **do not compare yourself to others** applies to climbing, it definitely feels bad when your friends can do a move a lot more easily than you can.

### Grade chasing

There is something that I definitely did when I first started out - try to get the highest grade possible. What this meant was that I was focusing on things that I was already good at. I excelled at crimpy slabs that involved high heel hooks or a lot of flexibility - if I were to send my first v8, that would likely be the style of boulder that I worked on. 

On the other hand, for styles I was bad at (e.g. big slopey holds, big burly moves) - boulders of that style I would sometimes climb 2 or even 3 grades lower than my limit (e.g. I struggled incredibly on a V4 dyno boulder over multiple sessions, while I sent a v6 crimpy one that was in my style in a few attempts).

### Dread is a signal

Have you ever looked at a new boulder set and gravitated towards certain boulders, while dreading to work on others? That dread is an asset. That dread is a signal. It is telling you information - that you don't want to work on it because it is something you are weak on.

### When you work on your weaknesses, you become a better all-around climber

When you work on things that you don't want to work on, the progress you make (and the new tools you gain!) will serve you in future climbs. Furthermore, the confidence that you build will be a positive feedback loop - you'll see the gains you've made, be enheartened, and want to tackle more boulders of that style.
